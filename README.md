# Master_Thesis_Log_Regression
This thesis analyses the drivers of foreign market exit intensity of Western MNCs (US, UK, EU) from Russia after Russia's attacks on Ukraine. The 2022 invasion of Ukraine provides a contemporary and well-documented setting in which the full range of exit responses can be observed. While prior research mainly focused on a binary exit-or-stay outcome and on why firms exit and how fast they do so, this thesis aims to explain how intensively they withdraw. To fill this gap, it models exit as an ordinal outcome across the five-level Yale CELI scale (Digging In, Buying Time, Scaling Back, Suspension, and Withdrawal) for 543 firms, using cumulative link (ordered logit) regression. The vulnerability framework of Dai et al. (2017) provides the underlying structure, with added extensions for regulatory pressure and financial resilience. The model then reports average marginal effects and odds ratios for economic exposure, sanctions, structural embeddedness (at-risk resources), and financial resilience. For this dataset, the results imply that economic exposure does not significantly predict exit intensity. Instead, sanction exposure acts as an external driver elevating intensity, and at-risk resources lower it. Financial resilience seems to play only a minor role, with only short-term liquidity tending to lower exit intensity. Overall, the findings suggest that exit intensity depends less on the scale of an MNC's commercial ties to Russia , but by the kind of exposure it carries.
